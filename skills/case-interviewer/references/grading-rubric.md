# Case interview grading rubric

The rubric the interviewer scores against at debrief time. Grounded in the LOMS strong-vs-weak transcript pairs (Victor Cheng's own inline commentary across Cases 1-8), cross-checked against how real MBB interviewers describe their scoring (ex-Bain, ex-BCG, ex-McKinsey accounts) and against the skeptic literature on interview noise. Sources and evidence trails live at the bottom.

## How to use it

Score each of the four dimensions 1-10 using the behavioral anchors below. Every score must cite specific moments from the transcript as evidence: quote the candidate or point to the exchange. Then apply the gate checks, then issue the overall verdict. Finish with "what a strong answer would have looked like": a concrete walkthrough of the case done well, not generic advice.

Two scoring realities to keep in mind, straight from practitioners:

- Real interviewers form a fast holistic judgment ("would I put this person in front of a client?") and the scorecard documents it afterward. The rubric exists to force the evidence-gathering that a gut call skips, so cite moments, don't just rate vibes.
- A single interviewer's read is noisy (roughly coin-flip reliability between candidates per Kahneman's noise research). Give scores with a stated confidence, and when a score is borderline, say what evidence would have settled it.

## Dimension 1: Structure (weight ~30%)

The heaviest-weighted dimension in every practitioner account. The core question: is the candidate running a hypothesis-driven, prunable structure, or wandering?

| Score | Anchors |
|---|---|
| 9-10 | States the objective in their own words and confirms it before analyzing. States a hypothesis up front, unprompted. Lays out a multi-layer structure (branches AND sub-branches) tailored to this case, explains why each branch matters and how its answer would change the recommendation. Numbers their points. Closes branches explicitly ("X that out") before moving on. Structure could be redrawn as a clean issue tree by an observer. |
| 6-8 | Clear framework stated before data requests, mostly tailored, hypothesis present even if not named. Occasional unnarrated jumps or a branch left open. Justifies factors when asked, not always proactively. |
| 4-5 | Generic memorized framework (customers/product/company/competition) marched through mechanically regardless of relevance. No stated hypothesis; questions arrive one at a time with no visible plan. Interviewer can't tell why any question is being asked. |
| 1-3 | No structure at all, or structure abandoned at first contact with data. Jumps between unrelated ideas driven by guessing. Analyzes the wrong objective entirely. |

Key tells from LOMS:
- Cheng passes or fails candidates on whether jumping around is hypothesis-driven and narrated versus intuition-driven and silent. The surface behavior looks identical; the narration is the difference (Case 2 Ex1 vs Ex2).
- The single most avoidable killer: solving the wrong problem. Strong candidates disambiguate near-identical goals ("increase sales, or increase profit?") before starting (Case 2 Ex2 p.2).
- An undiagrammable structure is itself evidence of a weak structure. Cheng only published issue-tree answer keys for the best-practice transcripts; the rest were "too hard to follow to diagram."
- In interviewer-led format, don't penalize the candidate for not front-loading the whole case structure; score the sub-structure of the question currently live. In interviewee-led format, the full blueprint up front matters.

## Dimension 2: Quantitative accuracy (weight ~20%)

Not arithmetic perfection: process, self-checking, and interpretation. Practitioners report 80-90% numeric accuracy is fine when the logic is narrated cleanly.

| Score | Anchors |
|---|---|
| 9-10 | Writes the formula in words before plugging in numbers. Reads the exhibit setup carefully and captures constraints before computing. Sanity-checks results against real-world plausibility ("double sales in a shrinking market? not likely"). Asks for every number two ways: versus its own history, versus competitors. Quantifies magnitudes, not just directions. Interprets data (draws the planted conclusion) rather than reciting it. Flags simplifying assumptions out loud. |
| 6-8 | Math right or a slip caught and corrected calmly on their own. Setup mostly careful. Some comparisons requested. May accept a number at face value once or twice. |
| 4-5 | Needs the interviewer to catch errors. Directional statements never quantified. Reads exhibits back verbatim with no interpretation. Gets bogged down in precision before knowing if it matters. |
| 1-3 | Misreads the problem setup and produces a materially wrong answer, uncaught. Repeats the same category of careless error. Freezes on estimation with no structured buildup. |

Key tells from LOMS:
- A math error from misreading the problem (not from arithmetic) is near-automatic rejection in Cheng's telling: "95 times out of 100 you're going to get rejected off the math" (Case 8 Ex1). See gate checks.
- A caught-and-corrected slip scores HIGHER than mechanical perfection in practitioner accounts: composure under a live error is what's being watched.
- A minor unflagged slip in an otherwise excellent performance costs very little (Case 1 Ex1: even Cheng missed it live).

## Dimension 3: Insight (weight ~25%)

Business judgment: does the candidate extract what the data means for the client's decision, or just process it?

| Score | Anchors |
|---|---|
| 9-10 | Reacts to surprising data by stopping and digging ("that's interesting, so what we offer isn't what customers want...") - this is where cases crack. Requests the 80/20 breakdown (profit by segment) before deep-diving anywhere. Outcome-level conclusions from exhibits, stated early. Mini-synthesis every few minutes tying data back to the client question. Raises the factor the case deliberately omitted (e.g. cost when the ask was only about sales). Second-order effects (cannibalization, brand dilution) surfaced unprompted. Discards quantitatively implausible levers decisively. |
| 6-8 | Reaches sound conclusions through a repeatable process. Some so-whats volunteered, some extracted by prompting. Surprising data noticed, though not always chased. |
| 4-5 | Registers surprises as "interesting" and moves on. Dives deep into a segment that isn't where the money is. Stays strictly inside the literal question. Second-order effects only after heavy prompting. |
| 1-3 | Right answer by lucky guessing (score it down: the process wouldn't survive an unsupervised client engagement, which is exactly why Cheng fails these candidates). Pet idea confirmed rather than tested. Claims that could equally support the opposite conclusion. |

Key tells from LOMS:
- Reaction-to-surprise is the highest-signal single moment in a case. Anchor scoring on it (Case 2 Ex1 vs Ex2 hit the same juncture with opposite behaviors).
- "Right answer" and "good process" are separate axes. Cheng explicitly declines to advance a candidate who landed the correct conclusion by guess-and-check.
- Award insight credit only for outcome statements ("this means Ralph Kline expands by 70 stores"), never for accurate restatement of the given numbers.

## Dimension 4: Communication (weight ~25%)

Practitioners call this the hardest dimension to coach and a frequent silent killer, especially passivity.

| Score | Anchors |
|---|---|
| 9-10 | Thinks out loud continuously; the interviewer always knows where they are and why. Takes deliberate silence to organize before big moments (structured pause beats rambling stream). Numbers spoken lists. Announces deviations from their own structure. Asks sharp, quantified questions ("what percentage", not "tell me about"). Closing synthesis leads with the answer in sentence one ("Yes, partner with Fashion World, for three reasons..."), then numbered reasons grounded in surfaced data, caveats last. Confident, client-facing tone with conviction. Recovers from redirects gracefully, without defensiveness. |
| 6-8 | Reasoning mostly narrated. Conclusion top-down but maybe unnumbered or thin on cited data. Tone workable but clinical or academic. Precise prompt wording tracked most of the time. |
| 4-5 | Long silent stretches of unexplained questions. Vague, open-ended asks. Synthesis is a chronological "diary recollection" of the analysis with the recommendation tacked on at the end. Wishy-washy close with no committed answer until pressed. |
| 1-3 | Passive: waits for prompts, never drives ("limited personal impact" in scorecard language). Needs repeated interviewer interventions and repeats the same failure after each. Misstates case terms and never notices the drift. |

Key tells from LOMS:
- The top-down conclusion is the most binary-checkable item in the whole rubric. Cheng: ~90% of candidates do it backwards, and doing it right is one of the clearest markers of the best candidates (Case 2 Ex2 pp.15-16).
- Two-notes discipline (clean issue-tree page shown to the interviewer, separate scratch page for math) is directly observable from uploaded notes and named by Cheng as a differentiator.

## Gate checks (apply after dimension scoring)

These override or cap the dimension math, because that's how real interviewers treat them:

1. **Misread-math gate.** A materially wrong answer from misreading the setup, uncaught by the candidate, caps the overall verdict at No hire unless literally everything else was exceptional (Cheng's 95-in-100 rule).
2. **Illogical-claim gate.** A claim that could equally support the opposite conclusion from the same facts, asserted with confidence, is grounds for rejection on its own: it reads as fabrication to a client (Case 8 Ex1).
3. **Wrong-problem gate.** If the candidate analyzed a different objective than the one given and never caught it, cap at No hire regardless of execution quality.
4. **Hint accounting.** Hints are not free. Note every hint given (the interviewer only gives them when asked, so this is trackable), and what the candidate did with each. Clean, immediate leverage of a hint is coachability (mild positive within an otherwise strong interview); needing hints to make any forward progress caps the verdict at Round 1 borderline.
5. **Intervention count.** Count interviewer rescues (redirects, stop-and-restarts). Zero or one with clean recovery is normal. Multiple interventions with the same failure repeating is a fail signal on its own (Case 4 Ex2).

## Overall verdict

Weighted average as a starting point (Structure 30, Insight 25, Communication 25, Quant 20), then gates, then place the candidate on Cheng's actual verdict tiers:

- **Strong hire**: the Case 8 Ex3 profile. No glaring errors on the things that matter (hypothesis, structure, math, synthesis, tone); gaps limited to subtle misses. Proactive everywhere a borderline candidate needed prompting.
- **Hire**: strong-but-real performance, the Case 1 Ex1 / Case 3 Ex3 anchor. Sound process, minor execution nits (unquantified trend, unnumbered reasons, small flagged slips).
- **Round 1 pass, final-round fail**: a real and common tier. Raw analytical capability clearly present ("could you do the job? no doubt") but polish is reactive instead of proactive: justifications extracted by prompting, synthesis data-rich but indecisive.
- **No hire**: any gate tripped, or wandering structure plus passive drive, or right answer via lucky process.

Calibration note: the "best practice" LOMS transcripts (Case 2 Ex2, Case 4 Ex3, Case 7 Ex3) are Cheng interviewing himself. Calibrate a 10 against them, but calibrate Hire against the best real candidates (Case 1 Ex1, Case 3 Ex3, Case 8 Ex3), or the scale drifts too harsh.

## Debrief output format

1. Verdict tier + one-sentence "client test" judgment (would you put them in front of a client?).
2. Four dimension scores with 2-4 evidence bullets each, quoting transcript moments (including uploaded notes/math where relevant).
3. Gate check results, hint ledger, intervention count.
4. **What a strong answer would have looked like**: walk the actual case, beat by beat - the opening structure a 9 would have laid out (drawn as the issue tree), the key data requests in order, the surprise moment and the dig it deserved, the math done with worded formulas, and the top-down closing synthesis verbatim, so the candidate can see the gap concretely.
5. Two or three prioritized drills for next time (specific and behavioral: "state the hypothesis before your framework", not "improve structure").

## Honesty caveats (from the skeptic lens, keep in the doc)

- No controlled study validates that any of this predicts consulting job performance. The apparatus runs on face validity and tradition. It is still the game as actually played.
- Case and fit halves gate each other at real firms; a strong case doesn't offset a weak fit read. This rubric scores the case half only.
- Mechanical recitation of a named framework is a negative signal, not a scoring line. Nothing here awards points for naming a framework.
